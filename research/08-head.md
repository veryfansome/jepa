# The `head` Chunk: Prediction Heads and Auxiliary Self-Supervised Objectives on a Shared Trunk

## Abstract

The `head` chunk of terminal-JEPA governs two orthogonal levers on top of a shared trunk: the **readout** that maps the trunk's hidden state `h` to the predicted next-observation embedding, and an optional **train-only auxiliary loss** that shapes the representation for downstream use — here, latent planning and multi-step rollout. This report surveys the main theoretical families behind these two levers: projection/prediction heads in self-supervised learning (SimCLR's discarded projector, the BYOL/SimSiam predictor, and the "head absorbs invariance" analyses); auxiliary tasks and multi-task representation shaping (UNREAL-style auxiliaries, negative transfer, and gradient conflict/PCGrad); model-based-RL readouts and latent rollouts (Dreamer, MuZero, TD-MPC, and scheduled-sampling/DAgger consistency); and inverse dynamics, successor representations, and reachability/quasimetric value learning — exactly the auxiliary families terminal-JEPA implemented. Across the literature and the project's own R7/R10/R11 rounds, a consistent picture emerges: heads and auxiliaries reliably *accelerate* learning and *protect* the trunk, but they do not reliably *raise the asymptote*, and terminal-JEPA's passthrough readout with a budget-limited trunk proved a strong baseline that no multi-horizon, rollout-consistency, inverse-dynamics, reachability, or successor auxiliary could beat at full budget.

## 1. The role of the head in this world model

In terminal-JEPA the trunk is a causal transformer (plus, in the champion lineage, delta-rule fast-weight memories) over an interleaved `cmd, obs, cmd, obs, …` stream of frozen encoder embeddings; the hidden state at each command position is read out into a predicted next-observation latent, scored by retrieval against hard same-verb foils. The `head` axis was added in R7 as a wrapper with a three-function contract (`evolve/CLAUDE.md`): `wrap(net, D)` may re-point `net.forward` and register readout/aux parameters — and must run **before** the optimizer is built so its parameters are optimized; `aux_loss(...)` is a **train-only** term returning a hard `0.0` for the passthrough (so `main + aux` is bit-identical to main); and `leak_safe(...)` is asserted before scoring.

The contract encodes two hard-won hazards. First, a **parent↔child module cycle**: because the head is added as a submodule of the base net, registering the base net back on the head creates infinite recursion in `.to()`/`.parameters()`, so the wrapper stores the base by a non-registered reference (`object.__setattr__`, seen in `r7_normres_multihorizon_aux.py`). Second, **forward recursion**: the trunk fallback must call the *original* bound forward saved before re-pointing, never `self.base(...)` (now the wrapped forward). A subtler, load-bearing caveat is the **arch bypass**: the head recomputes the prediction from the trunk `h`, so on an architecture whose prediction is *not* a per-position function of `h` (e.g. the hippocampal episodic read) a `pred = head(h)` readout silently bypasses that mechanism. This is why the R7 head result had to be isolated on a `pred=head(h)` arch — and why the later R10/R11 auxiliary heads adopt a **no-module, forward-untouched** design (`wrap` adds nothing and returns a config dict; the aux touches only training), which composes with any arch and keeps `net.state_dict()` arch-pure so the frozen planning instruments can load checkpoints strictly.

Leak-safety is the invariant the whole search trusts. The main readout must be a strictly per-position function of `h` (LayerNorm + Linear + per-position MLP — no cross-token op), so `obs_t` cannot influence the `cmd_≤t` prediction; every auxiliary consumes only past inputs and the net's own causal outputs, with true future observations entering **only as loss labels** inside `aux_loss`, which is absent from `forward()` and thus dropped at eval.

## 2. Projection and prediction heads in self-supervised learning

The single most influential head result is SimCLR's: inserting a small **nonlinear projection head** MLP between the backbone representation and the contrastive (InfoNCE) loss, then **discarding it** for downstream tasks, "substantially improves the quality of the learned representations," and the pre-projection representation transfers better than the post-projection one [1]. This is counterintuitive — we throw away the layers the loss was actually applied to — and it has motivated a line of analysis into *why*. The dominant explanation is that **the head absorbs the invariances the pretext loss demands**. A contrastive loss with (say) color-jitter augmentations pressures its input to become color-invariant; if that pressure landed on the backbone it would destroy class-relevant features, but a disposable projection head acts as a sacrificial preconditioner that soaks up the extreme invariance/distortion demand and shields the backbone, which is then free to retain more generalizable, less-invariant features [4]. Formally, Gupta et al. show the projection head implicitly performs a **data-dependent subspace selection**: it chooses a feature subspace on which to enforce the contrastive objective, letting the backbone keep information the loss would otherwise discard; they argue this emergent behavior should be made explicit via a bilevel objective [4].

The **predictor** in non-contrastive SSL plays a related but distinct anti-collapse role. BYOL trains an online network to predict the target network's representation of another augmentation of the same image, with no negative pairs; an extra **predictor MLP** on the online branch plus a slow-moving-average target is what prevents the trivial constant solution [2]. SimSiam strips this to its core — no negatives, no momentum, no large batches — and isolates the mechanism: a predictor plus a **stop-gradient** is essential and sufficient to avoid collapse, which the authors frame as an implicit expectation-maximization / alternating optimization [3]. The lesson terminal-JEPA inherits: a head is not merely a bigger readout — it mediates *what geometry the loss imposes on the trunk*, and a poorly matched head can either protect the trunk (SimCLR) or, if fused into the shared representation, damage it.

Terminal-JEPA's R7 head (`r7_normres_multihorizon_aux.py`) instantiates exactly this template — a normalized-residual MLP main readout (identity-initialized refinement) plus a multi-horizon auxiliary Linear predicting `k=2,3`-step future observations, dropped at eval — and it produced **no gain** over its clean-isolation passthrough control. The passthrough head (the arch's own Linear readout, no aux) is the strong baseline.

## 3. Auxiliary tasks and multi-task representation shaping

The canonical demonstration that self-supervised **auxiliary tasks** accelerate RL is UNREAL [5]: an A3C agent augmented with pixel-control (learn to maximally change patches of the input), reward-prediction, and value-function-replay pseudo-rewards, all sharing one representation, reaches ~87% of expert-human score on Labyrinth (DeepMind Lab) — and ~880% on Atari — while learning ~10× faster than A3C [5]. The mechanism is representation shaping under sparse reward: auxiliary targets supply dense gradient that carves useful features when the primary signal is thin.

But auxiliaries are **not free**, and the literature is explicit that they can hurt. Empirically the results are mixed — pixel-control, reward-prediction and next-state-prediction help substantially in some environments and marginally or negatively in others [5, 18]. The clean mechanistic account is **gradient conflict**: when the auxiliary and main gradients point in opposing directions (negative cosine similarity) — especially with large magnitude differences and high curvature — the shared parameters receive destructive interference. PCGrad ("gradient surgery") formalizes this and mitigates it by projecting each task's gradient onto the normal plane of any conflicting task's gradient, keeping shared positive directions while removing antagonistic components, yielding better data efficiency across multi-task, scene-understanding, and RL benchmarks [6]. Lyle et al. analyze the **representation dynamics** auxiliaries induce and show that whether an auxiliary helps depends on how its features align with and accelerate the main task's own learning, not on the auxiliary's intrinsic difficulty [19].

This frames terminal-JEPA's R10/R11 negative results precisely: an auxiliary whose gradient is well-aligned with the retrieval margin (the fitness) will help; one that competes for capacity will, at best, wash out at convergence. The project's fitness — content-verb margin — measures *differential* help over lexical baselines, so an auxiliary that lifts the world model and its baselines equally, or that trades margin for planning fidelity, cannot promote. Notably the champion lineage already tolerates a small MSE anchor without margin damage, so the ceiling is not "any auxiliary breaks fitness" but "no tried auxiliary raised the asymptote."

## 4. Model-based-RL readouts and latent rollouts

World models are, architecturally, a bundle of heads on a shared latent trunk, and the design of those heads is central. **MuZero** learns a latent dynamics model with three readouts — a **reward** head, a **policy** head, and a **value** head — trained end-to-end so the latent predicts *only the quantities relevant to planning*, not the observation itself, and plans by tree search in that latent [8]. This "value-equivalence" stance — read out what planning consumes — is the deep justification for terminal-JEPA's reachability and successor auxiliaries: shape the latent so the *planning readout* (here, cosine-to-goal) is well-behaved. **Dreamer** instead learns behaviors by **latent imagination**, rolling the world model forward in a compact latent state and backpropagating analytic value gradients through the imagined trajectory [7]; **TD-MPC** combines a task-oriented latent dynamics model for short-horizon trajectory optimization with a TD-learned terminal value, achieving strong sample efficiency and asymptotic performance [9]. All three make the same bet terminal-JEPA makes: plan in latent space, and train the readouts so latent rollouts are trustworthy.

The failure mode of latent rollouts is **compounding error under the model's own outputs** — the exposure-bias problem. A model trained only under teacher forcing never consumes its own predictions, so at rollout time inputs come from a distribution it never saw, and errors accumulate. Scheduled sampling [14] and DAgger [15] are the classic fixes: expose the model to its own outputs during training (curriculum-mixed for scheduled sampling; via a no-regret dataset-aggregation reduction for DAgger). Terminal-JEPA's `r10_rollout_write_consistency_aux.py` and `r11_replay_successor_rollout_codex.py` are direct latent-space instantiations: on a row-subsample, feed the model's own obs-space prediction back into the stream (the "write" imagination policy that live latent-MPC planning must use), then MSE/cosine-penalize the next-step prediction against the *true* next observation. This was motivated by a measured defect — the champion's imagined listings are grossly off-manifold (‖pred‖²/‖true‖² ≈ 4.7; matched sqL2 ≈ 2020 > random-pair ≈ 1430) because the contrastive row-softmax is invariant to each prediction's own norm. The rollout-consistency head *did* supply the norm-calibration gradient, and the R11 chained-rollout head produced striking **early-budget** planning gains (remaining-depth-4 accuracy 0.011 → 0.286 at proxy) — but these **inverted at full budget** (paired batteries 0/3 seeds positive), and no variant promoted.

## 5. Inverse dynamics, successor representations, and reachability

These three families are the auxiliaries terminal-JEPA tried most deliberately, each with a real literature.

**Inverse dynamics** learns action-relevant features by predicting the action that produced a transition. Agrawal et al.'s "learning to poke by poking" jointly trains forward and inverse models, where the inverse objective supplies supervision that shapes informative visual features and the forward model regularizes them [11]. Pathak et al.'s ICM builds its feature space with an inverse-dynamics model precisely so the representation encodes only what the *action* can affect, ignoring uncontrollable nuisance variation [10]. The planning rationale is sharp: a forward-prediction loss alone lets the predictor under-use the action and drift toward the context's mean continuation, so imagined outcomes of different actions become indistinguishable and argmin-over-actions planning degenerates to noise. Terminal-JEPA's `r11_invdyn_action_witness_aux.py` implements an **action-witness InfoNCE**: a private decoder must identify *which command* produced `[prev_obs; pred_t]` among in-batch commands, with the only gradient into the trunk flowing through `pred_t`, forcing action-injectivity on exactly the imagination the planning battery ranks. It aimed at the measured remaining-depth-1 sibling-discrimination failure (up to ~237 `cd` siblings share one history). It did not promote.

**Successor representations (SR)** and **successor features (SF)** decouple environment dynamics from reward. Dayan's SR represents a state by the discounted expected future occupancy of other states, so states with similar *successors* generalize together — TD machinery learns the representation itself, not just a value [12]. Barreto et al.'s SF generalize this to function approximation, factoring value into a reward-independent successor-feature vector and a reward weight, enabling zero-shot transfer across tasks that share dynamics [13]. Terminal-JEPA's `r11_replay_successor_rollout_codex.py` takes the hippocampal-replay reading of this idea — train on short internally generated successor chains rather than single one-step transitions — writing 2–3 self-predicted observations forward and supervising the successor prediction. The intuition matches SR's "represent where you're going," but on the frozen substrate it too failed to lift full-budget planning.

**Reachability / temporal-distance / quasimetric** value learning makes latent distance *mean time-to-reach*. The optimal goal-conditioned value is the shortest path length, which is a quasimetric (asymmetric, triangle-inequality-respecting); QRL restricts the value to a quasimetric network and learns it directly, exploiting the triangle inequality [17]. Time-Contrastive Networks learn embeddings where temporal neighbors attract and distant frames repel, so distance tracks time [16]. Terminal-JEPA's `r11_reachability_horizon_rank_aux.py` is a **parameter-free time-contrastive ranking**: it enforces that each prediction's cosine to its trajectory's true future observations be *monotone in horizon* (near > far, both forward and goal-anchored), a discrete value-shaping potential so that at a filesystem fan-out the on-path child's imagination cosine-dominates off-path siblings. This is the most literature-faithful of the auxiliaries — and, like the rest, it did not raise the asymptote.

## 6. Why auxiliaries help early but not at convergence

The clearest cross-cutting finding — in both the literature and terminal-JEPA — is that an auxiliary can **accelerate** learning without raising the **asymptote**. UNREAL's headline is a ~10× *speed-up* to a similar-or-higher score [5]; Lyle et al. show auxiliaries act largely on representation *dynamics* — how fast useful features form — rather than on the fixed point [19]; and Voelcker et al.'s recent analysis of self-prediction auxiliaries makes the distinction explicit, characterizing *when* self-prediction and observation-prediction auxiliaries improve the learned representation and *when* they are redundant with what the main objective already learns [18]. A training signal that supplies gradient the main loss lacks *early* (dense targets, exposure to own outputs, action-injectivity) can be fully subsumed once the main objective, given enough budget, has learned the same structure — at which point the auxiliary is at best neutral and at worst a competing gradient.

This is exactly terminal-JEPA's R11 record: chained rollout-consistency lifted remaining-depth-4 planning accuracy 0.011 → 0.286 *at proxy*, but the champion's *own* planning accuracy grows 0.36 → 0.60 with training budget — so **budget itself was the largest planning lever measured**, the early-budget auxiliary gains **inverted at full budget** (0/3 seeds positive), and the R10+R11 pair became the recorded evidence that training-signal auxiliaries on the frozen substrate do not move full-budget planning, pointing instead at a world/representation expansion (v2) as the binding lever. The passthrough head remains the incumbent.

## 7. Design guidance and open questions for future `head` impls

1. **Beat the passthrough, honestly.** The arch's own Linear readout, no aux, is a strong baseline; every head must clear its *clean-isolation* control, and on a `pred=head(h)` arch to avoid silently bypassing a non-per-position trunk mechanism (the R7 arch-bypass caveat).
2. **Prefer forward-untouched, no-module auxiliaries.** The R10/R11 pattern (`wrap` returns a config dict, adds nothing to `net`) sidesteps both hazards, composes with any arch, and keeps checkpoints arch-pure for frozen instruments — a strictly safer default than re-pointing `forward`.
3. **Align the auxiliary gradient with fitness, and check for conflict.** Fitness rewards *differential* margin over lexical baselines; an auxiliary that lifts baselines equally, or trades margin for calibration, cannot promote. PCGrad-style conflict diagnostics [6] would make "is this aux fighting the main loss?" measurable rather than inferred.
4. **Expect acceleration, test the asymptote.** Given [5, 18, 19] and the R11 inversion, screen auxiliaries at *full* budget with paired seeds; an early-budget proxy win is necessary, not sufficient, and here proxy over-promised.
5. **Open question — is the readout even the right lever?** The R10/R11 conclusion is that the binding constraint is the *substrate* (frozen encoder, one-step-supervised trunk, near-empty `cd` renders), not the readout. A head that changes *what is predicted* (e.g. a MuZero-style value-equivalent readout [8] trained against a genuine planning target, or a quasimetric-structured latent [17]) rather than merely *how `h` is decoded* is the more promising, and untested, direction — but likely only in tandem with a richer world (v2).
