# The `batcher` Chunk: Negative-Pool Composition, Hard-Negative Mining, and Curriculum for a Retrieval-Evaluated Contrastive World Model

## Abstract

The terminal-JEPA world model is trained contrastively and evaluated by hard-negative retrieval: at each held-out step the true next observation must be ranked above **same-verb, same-system foils**. The `batcher` chunk controls the one degree of freedom that most directly shapes this — *which sequences co-occur in a training batch*, and therefore what the in-batch negative pool looks like. This report surveys the main theoretical families that govern negative-pool composition for contrastively-trained, retrieval-evaluated models: (i) in-batch negatives and the role of negative *count* (SimCLR, MoCo, CPC/InfoNCE, and the mutual-information bounds behind them); (ii) hard-negative mining, from classical metric-learning batch-hard/semi-hard through hard-negative sampling and mixing in self-supervised learning, and the false-negative/sampling-bias hazard that bounds how hard one can safely go; (iii) dense-retrieval negative strategies (DPR in-batch, ANCE ANN-mined, RocketQA cross-batch and denoised), which are the most on-domain body of work because the fitness here *is* retrieval; and (iv) curriculum/self-paced ordering and experience-replay-style batch construction. Throughout I tie the theory to what this project actually measured: a system-blocked hard-negative curriculum that aligns the training negative distribution to the eval's foils beat uniform batching and trended up with harder blocking, while collision-clique and memory-exercising replay curricula landed at or below it.

## 1. The batcher's role in this world model

The contract is deliberately narrow. `make_batcher(fit, bs, seed) -> next_batch(step, total)` returns exactly `bs` integer indices into the read-only training pool on each step; it may inspect metadata (`image`, `cmds`, cached `z_cmd`) but must be deterministic given `seed`, must not touch the global RNG, and — critically — never touches predictions, targets, the loss, or the eval. It changes *only which sequences are batched together*.

That narrowness is exactly why it matters. The model is trained with an L2-normalized InfoNCE-family objective (later a free-energy precision-weighted contrastive loss), so the loss at a command position pushes the predicted next-observation embedding toward its true continuation and away from *the other observations in the same batch*. The batch **is** the negative distribution. Meanwhile fitness is a retrieval margin on **content verbs** (`ls`/`cat`): rank the true next observation against 63 **same-verb** foils drawn from the two-image inner-val pool, so the decisive foils are same-verb *and* same-system, and increasingly same-subtree. Uniform iid batching makes most in-batch negatives *cross-system*, which a cheap "which distro is this" shortcut separates — a feature that is useless at test time, where all foils share the system. The batcher's job is to close the gap between the *train-time* negative distribution and the *test-time* foil distribution. This is the train/test negative-alignment principle that unifies every family below, made concrete for a shell world model.

## 2. Theoretical frameworks for negative sampling and curriculum

### 2.1 In-batch negatives and the role of negative count

The InfoNCE loss originates with Contrastive Predictive Coding [1]: given a context, a positive is drawn from the true conditional `p(x|c)` and `N−1` negatives from a proposal `p(x)`, and the softmax-over-similarities objective is a variational lower bound on the mutual information between context and target, with the bound value capped near `log N`. This is the formal reason "more negatives" helps: a larger `N` raises the ceiling of the MI bound and yields a lower-variance estimate of the contrast, sharpening the representation — but with diminishing, `log`-scaled returns and a genuine bias/variance trade-off, analyzed carefully by Poole et al. [2], who show these bounds degrade (high bias or high variance) when the true MI is large. So negative *count* is a real lever, not an unbounded one.

SimCLR [3] operationalizes in-batch negatives at scale: every other augmented example in the minibatch serves as a negative, and the paper's headline empirical finding is that contrastive learning benefits disproportionately from **large batches and long training** — because the batch size directly sets the negative count. MoCo [4] attacks the same need from the other side: rather than pay the memory cost of enormous batches, it decouples the dictionary of negatives from the batch via a **queue** of embeddings from recent batches, kept consistent by a momentum-updated key encoder — a large, cheap negative pool. Wang & Isola [5] give the mechanistic account of *what* those negatives buy: the contrastive loss asymptotically optimizes **alignment** (positives close) and **uniformity** (features spread on the hypersphere), and negatives are precisely the force driving uniformity. For terminal-JEPA the takeaway is that batch composition is not just "how many" negatives but *where on the relevant manifold they sit*: uniformity pressure spent separating trivially-different cross-system observations is wasted; the eval rewards uniformity *within* a system.

### 2.2 Hard-negative mining

If the negatives that matter are the confusable ones, sample those. Classical metric learning established both the payoff and the hazard. FaceNet [6] introduced triplet loss with in-**batch** mining, but found that *hardest*-negative selection with large batches drives collapse, and so used **semi-hard** negatives (farther than the positive but still inside the margin) — the first statement of "hard, but not too hard." Hermans et al. [7], "In Defense of the Triplet Loss," made the now-standard **batch-hard** recipe: sample P identities × K instances per batch and, for each anchor, take the hardest positive and hardest negative *within the batch* — cheap, effective, and the direct ancestor of blocked-batch hard-negative construction. The lesson terminal-JEPA inherits: hardness should come from *batch composition* (who is in the batch), not from a separate global mining pass.

Self-supervised contrastive learning imported these ideas but had to solve them without labels. Robinson et al. [8] derive an **unsupervised hard-negative sampling** distribution that up-weights negatives close to the anchor, with a user-controllable hardness knob, and show sharper representations and faster convergence. Kalantidis et al. [9] (MoCHi) observe that even MoCo's queue rarely contains truly hard negatives late in training and **synthesize** them by mixing the hardest existing negatives in feature space — "hard negative mixing" — for cheap, on-the-fly hardness. Both confirm that *harder negatives sharpen the boundary*, echoing this project's "harder blocking trended up."

The counter-force is **false negatives / sampling bias**. Chuang et al. [10] formalize it: with no labels, uniformly-sampled negatives sometimes share the anchor's latent class, and InfoNCE then *repels embeddings that should attract*. Their **debiased** objective corrects the negative distribution to approximate sampling from a true-negative marginal. This is the principled ceiling on hardness: the harder you mine, the more likely a "negative" is actually a near-positive. In terminal-JEPA the risk is concrete — two `cat /etc/os-release` reads on the same distro, or two `ls /` listings, are near-identical *true* continuations, so an all-hard batch of same-key sequences risks manufacturing false negatives. This is exactly why the champion batcher uses a hard/easy **mix** with a ramp, never all-hard — the "ratio, not maximal hardness" design point, and it is consistent with why the fully-specific **collision-clique** curriculum did not beat the coarser image-block.

### 2.3 Dense-retrieval negative strategies (the most on-domain family)

Because fitness here is retrieval, dense passage retrieval is the closest prior art. DPR [11] trains a dual encoder with **in-batch negatives** — every other question's gold passage is a negative — plus a small number of BM25 "hard" negatives (lexically similar but wrong); the in-batch trick makes the effective negative count scale with batch size at near-zero cost, and adding *one* BM25 hard negative per question gave a large lift. This is the template terminal-JEPA follows: cheap in-batch negatives, densified toward the hard region.

ANCE [12] states the alignment principle most sharply: **the training negatives are not representative of the irrelevant documents seen at test time**, and this train/test mismatch — not model capacity — is the bottleneck. ANCE fixes it by mining negatives from an **asynchronously-updated approximate-nearest-neighbor index of the whole corpus**, so negatives are, by construction, the current model's hardest confusions and resemble test-time distractors. Terminal-JEPA's system-blocking is a cheap, static, metadata-driven surrogate for ANCE's expensive dynamic mining: instead of an ANN index, it uses the *known* structure (same image ⇒ same system ⇒ eval-like foil) to densify the hard region without a nearest-neighbor pass.

RocketQA [13] contributes two refinements that map directly onto this project's open questions. **Cross-batch negatives** pool negatives across all batches on all GPUs, again raising effective negative count (the MoCo-queue idea for retrieval). **Denoised hard negatives** use a stronger cross-encoder to *remove* top-retrieved passages likely to be false negatives before using them — the retrieval-domain instantiation of debiasing [10], and direct evidence that in retrieval "hard" must be paired with "denoised."

### 2.4 Curriculum learning, self-paced learning, and difficulty-matching

Bengio et al. [14] formalized **curriculum learning**: presenting examples easy→hard can speed convergence and improve generalization, interpreted as a continuation method that starts on a smoothed objective and anneals toward the full, harder one. Kumar et al. [15] made difficulty *model-driven* with **self-paced learning**, selecting examples by current loss rather than a fixed hand-ordered schedule. The champion batcher is a curriculum in exactly Bengio's sense on a *composition* axis rather than a per-example one: the hard (same-system) fraction ramps from 0 to its maximum over the first ~30% of training, so early optimization sees an easy, well-separated negative pool and hardness anneals in — stabilizing the very regime where FaceNet [6] saw hard-mining collapse. A subtlety the project's own results underline: curriculum must serve *difficulty-matching to the test distribution*, not difficulty for its own sake. The **steady-state** distribution should equal the eval's foil geometry; the ramp is only a warm-up. Curricula that instead tilt the *steady-state* distribution toward something the eval does not score (memory-exercising replay, below) gave up the alignment the champion banks.

### 2.5 Replay-style batch construction

Two neuroscience-inspired families motivated the project's non-champion batchers. **Prioritized experience replay** [16] samples transitions with probability proportional to their TD-error, replaying "surprising" experience more often — the general principle of *non-uniform sampling weighted by learning signal*. **Similarity-weighted interleaved learning (SWIL)** [17], from complementary-learning-systems theory, interleaves new memories with the *representationally similar* subset of old ones to integrate knowledge without catastrophic interference. The loop-closure curriculum drew on Lu, Hasson & Norman [18], who show a memory-augmented network learns *when* to retrieve only when the training distribution actually contains recurring items — motivating oversampling sequences whose later commands re-query earlier ones, to feed the fast-weights arch's associative memory useful gradient. These are principled, but they optimize a *different* objective (exercising the arch's memory machinery, or preserving old structure) than the batcher's proven lever (aligning negatives to eval foils) — which is precisely what the results reflect.

## 3. Domain-specific findings, including this project's own

The project's `batcher` sweep is a clean, in-domain test of the theory above.

- **Uniform → system-blocked hard-negative curriculum was the win.** Densifying same-system in-batch negatives to match the eval's same-verb-same-system foils beat uniform batching. This is the ANCE/DPR train-test-alignment principle [11, 12] plus a Bengio ramp [14], and it is in the champion genome (`r6_sysblock_hardneg_curriculum`, block1/hard75/ramp30).
- **Harder blocking trended up, and single-image blocking is in the champion.** Blocking the hard fraction onto *fewer* images (down to one) raises same-system negative density further — consistent with [8, 9] that harder negatives sharpen the boundary — bounded by the hard/easy mix that guards against the false-negative regime [6, 10, 13].
- **The fully-specific collision-clique curriculum did *not* beat coarser image-blocking.** `r7_collision_clique_curriculum` sampled the hard block from `(verb, image, subtree-prefix)` cliques — negatives that are same-verb, same-system, *and* same-subtree, i.e. the most eval-exact geometry — yet landed at or below image-blocking. The likely mechanism is precisely the false-negative ceiling [10, 13]: at maximum specificity, "negatives" become near-identical true continuations, and the debiasing hazard cancels the sharper-boundary benefit.
- **Memory-exercising replay curricula were at or below.** `r8_loop_closure_memory_curriculum` (oversample loop-closure-rich sequences, tilt annealed to zero) [18] and `r8_swil_path_replay_curriculum` (similarity-weighted route-neighbor packets) [17] both kept the champion's hard/easy structure but added a replay lever aimed at the arch's memory rather than at the eval's foils — and neither beat the incumbent. Interpreted through §2.4/§2.5: they perturb the batch toward an objective the fitness does not score, so at best they are neutral on the metric that selects.

The through-line: for a retrieval-evaluated contrastive model, the batcher lever that pays is **negative-distribution alignment to the eval's hard foils**, held just short of the false-negative regime by a hard/easy mix and a curriculum ramp. Every batcher that instead optimized a proxy objective (subtree specificity, memory recurrence, interference avoidance) was dominated by the one that optimized alignment directly.

## 4. Design guidance and open questions for future `batcher` implementations

1. **Keep aligning to the foils, but make it dynamic.** System-blocking is a static surrogate for ANCE's ANN-mined negatives [12]. A model-in-the-loop batcher that periodically re-ranks candidate negatives by the *current* model's predicted-latent similarity — an ANCE-style refresh keyed on the frozen cmd/obs embeddings already cached — is the natural next step, and the untested one.
2. **Add explicit false-negative denoising before going harder.** Since specificity alone plateaued, the RocketQA move [13] is to *filter* near-duplicate true continuations out of the hard block (e.g. drop candidate negatives whose target embedding is within ε of the anchor's true next-obs), then push hardness up. This directly tests whether the collision-clique plateau was a debiasing failure [10].
3. **Raise effective negative count cheaply.** MoCo/RocketQA queues and cross-batch negatives [4, 13] increase `N` without larger batches; on a small-batch MPS/CUDA budget this is the cheapest route to a higher MI ceiling [1, 2] and is orthogonal to blocking.
4. **Treat the ramp as a hyperparameter, not a constant.** Self-paced difficulty [15] (schedule hardness by the model's current retrieval loss) could replace the fixed 30% ramp; whether model-driven pacing beats a hand-set ramp on this substrate is open.
5. **Beware epistasis with the arch.** The memory-exercising curricula target a specific arch's mechanism; their value may reappear only when *recombined* with an arch that can exploit the exercised memory — consistent with the program's "retry failed traits recombined" rule. The batcher's payoff is not arch-independent.

Open questions: Does dynamic ANN mining beat static blocking enough to justify its cost on a frozen-encoder substrate? Is there a measurable false-negative rate at which harder blocking inverts? And can a batcher that *both* aligns negatives to foils *and* exercises the arch's memory (a two-objective composition) exceed the alignment-only champion — the one combination the sweep has not yet isolated?
