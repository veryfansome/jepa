# Optimizers and Learning-Rate Schedules for a Small, Contrastively-Trained Sequence World Model

## Abstract

This report surveys the theories behind optimizer and learning-rate-schedule design for the `optim` chunk of the terminal-JEPA world model — a small transformer/fast-weights sequence model trained contrastively on frozen encoder features under a fixed, convergence-limited step budget. We cover adaptive first-order methods (SGD+momentum, Adam, AdamW) and why decoupled weight decay matters for adaptive optimizers; learning-rate schedules (warmup, cosine annealing/SGDR, floors and holds) and their interaction with batch size (the linear scaling rule, LARS/LAMB); weight-averaging methods (SWA, EMA) and why averaging finds flatter, better-generalizing minima; and structured/second-order-ish updates (Muon's Newton–Schulz orthogonalization, Shampoo, K-FAC/natural gradient) with an emphasis on why orthogonalizing updates to *associative-memory key matrices* is well motivated. We connect each family to what this project actually found: the first evolved-optimizer win was a Muon-style orthogonalizer scoped to the fast-weights key-addressing matrices, which landed only after the architecture changed to a delta-rule memory and only after a differently-targeted homeostatic optimizer failed; stochastic-weight-averaging underperformed in every context tried. Every citation was verified during writing; arXiv preprints and blog writeups are marked as such.

## 1. The role of the optimizer/schedule in this world model

The `optim` chunk's contract is deliberately narrow: `make(params, steps) -> (opt, sched)`, where the harness calls `opt.step()` then `sched.step()` once per iteration (`chunks/optim/baseline_adamw.py`). It controls *only* the optimization strategy — optimizer and LR schedule — not batch size, not the loss, not the eval. Three properties of the training regime shape what a good `optim` impl should do:

1. **Small model on frozen features.** Only a compact transformer / fast-weights trunk is learned (d≈176, key_d=64 in the champion); the encoder is frozen. Parameter counts are small and the loss landscape is dominated by a handful of matmul weight matrices, LayerNorm gains, and — in the champion — four thin `(key_d, d)` key-addressing projections.
2. **Contrastive objective.** Fitness is held-out next-observation retrieval; training uses an InfoNCE-style / free-energy-precision contrastive loss descended from Contrastive Predictive Coding [15]. Contrastive losses have curvature that shifts as the negative pool and temperature take effect, so the optimizer's preconditioner must adapt within budget rather than lock onto early-step statistics.
3. **Fixed, convergence-limited budget.** proxy = 1000 steps, full = 4000 steps, grad-norm clipped to 1.0. The recorded proxy→full improvement (e.g. InfoNCE 0.39→0.47) signals the regime is *under-optimized, not over-regularized*: reaching a good basin faster and not wasting the final steps at LR≈0 both help. This is the explicit rationale behind the best hand-designed schedule (`go_warmup_cosine_floor_beta98_decoupled.py`).

Because some parameters are associative-memory matrices, the "one update rule for all weights" assumption is not obviously right here — which is where structured/per-group updates (Section 3.4) become the interesting design axis.

## 2. Adaptive first-order methods and decoupled weight decay

**SGD with momentum** is the baseline of the field: the update is a running (exponentially-weighted) average of gradients, which damps oscillation across high-curvature directions and accelerates along consistent ones. It has one global step size and no per-coordinate scaling.

**Adam** [1] adds per-coordinate adaptive learning rates by dividing the first-moment estimate (momentum) by the square root of a second-moment (uncentered gradient variance) estimate, with bias correction. Adam is invariant to diagonal rescaling of gradients, needs little memory, and handles noisy/sparse/non-stationary objectives — exactly the profile of a small contrastive model whose gradient scale varies across the LayerNorm gains, the projection matmuls, and the key matrices. The β₂ choice is load-bearing under a short budget: the project's tuned schedule uses β₂=0.98 (≈50-step memory) rather than 0.999 (≈1000-step memory), because at 0.999 the second-moment estimate barely converges within a 1000-step proxy run and the preconditioner is effectively frozen (`go_warmup_cosine_floor_beta98_decoupled.py`).

**AdamW / decoupled weight decay** [2] is the correction that the whole `optim` chunk assumes. Loshchilov & Hutter showed that the standard way of implementing L2 regularization inside an adaptive optimizer — adding `λw` to the gradient — is *not* equivalent to true weight decay, because that penalty gradient then passes through Adam's per-coordinate `1/√v` rescaling. Weights with large historical gradients get their decay attenuated, coupling regularization strength to gradient history in an unintended way. Decoupling weight decay (applying `w ← w(1 − ηλ)` as a separate multiplicative step) restores the intended, uniform shrinkage, decouples the optimal `λ` from the optimal LR, and improves generalization for adaptive methods. Every evolved `optim` impl here uses AdamW, and the best hand-designed one goes further with the *standard no-decay-on-norm/bias rule*: decay only ≥2-D matmul/embedding weights, set `wd=0` on 1-D LayerNorm gains and biases (decaying a pre-LN net's norm gains would suppress signal). This is the widely-adopted parameter-group split first popularized for large-batch and transformer training.

## 3. Theoretical frameworks for schedule and update-rule design

### 3.1 Warmup: variance reduction in the early phase

Linear LR warmup — ramp from ~0 to peak over the first few percent of steps — is used by every non-baseline impl here (4–6% warmup). The clearest theory for *why* comes from Liu et al. [6]: the adaptive learning rate (Adam's `1/√v`) has pathologically high *variance* in the first few iterations because `v` is estimated from very few samples, so early updates are erratic and can push the model into a bad region it never escapes. Warmup acts as a variance-reduction mechanism, keeping steps small until the second-moment estimate stabilizes; their RAdam optimizer rectifies this variance analytically and, tellingly, behaves like Adam-with-warmup. Warmup was independently found essential for large-batch training [4] for a related reason — early gradients are large and poorly conditioned. Either way, warmup buys stability precisely where a fixed-budget contrastive run is most fragile.

### 3.2 Cosine annealing, SGDR, floors and holds

**SGDR** [3] introduced cosine annealing with warm restarts: decay the LR along a cosine curve from peak toward zero, optionally restarting. The cosine shape spends more steps at moderate-to-low LR (fine-tuning the basin) than a linear decay, and empirically improves anytime performance. The project's schedules adopt the cosine *shape* but modify the endpoints for a short budget in two ways grounded in the recorded search: (a) a **hold** at peak LR after warmup (20–30% of steps) before annealing, keeping the model in the high-LR exploration regime longer; and (b) a nonzero **floor** (decay to 0.05–0.1× peak, never to 0), because the proxy→full evidence says the last steps are still productive and annealing to zero wastes them. The champion optim (`r8_muon_key_orthogonal_addressing.py`) uses exactly this `warmup(4%) → hold(30%) → cosine-to-floor(peak/20)` trajectory for its AdamW group.

### 3.3 Batch size, the linear scaling rule, and large-batch optimizers

Schedule and optimizer interact with batch size. The **linear scaling rule** [4] — scale LR proportionally to batch size, with warmup to absorb the early instability — lets minibatch SGD reach large batches (ImageNet in 1 hour) without accuracy loss, on the intuition that a k× larger batch gives a k×-lower-variance gradient estimate that can support a k× larger step. The rule breaks down at extreme batch sizes, motivating **layer-wise adaptive rate scaling (LARS)** [5], which sets a per-layer LR proportional to the ratio of weight norm to gradient norm, and its Adam-based successor LAMB. These are directly relevant conceptually: LARS/LAMB are the first widely-used demonstration that *different parameter groups want different effective step sizes* — the same premise the project's per-group and structured optimizers rest on, even though terminal-JEPA trains at modest batch sizes where the global scaling rule itself is not the binding constraint.

### 3.4 Structured, orthogonalized, and second-order-ish updates

Adaptive first-order methods precondition with a *diagonal* approximation of curvature (one scalar per coordinate). A richer family estimates or imposes *matrix* structure on the update.

**K-FAC / natural gradient** [11] approximates natural gradient descent — preconditioning by the inverse Fisher information matrix, the curvature of the loss in distribution space rather than parameter space — using a Kronecker-factored approximation of each layer's Fisher block. This makes non-diagonal, non-low-rank curvature tractable and can make far more progress per step than momentum SGD. It is the theoretical anchor for "the geometry of the update matters, not just its coordinates."

**Shampoo** [10] is a practical structure-aware preconditioner: it maintains a separate preconditioning matrix per tensor dimension (contracting over the others), giving a full-matrix-like adaptive method whose per-step cost stays close to SGD/Adam. Shampoo and K-FAC both converge faster than diagonal methods on well-conditioned problems but carry extra compute/memory — often not worth it for a small model, which is why terminal-JEPA does not use them wholesale.

**Muon** is the recent, and for this project the decisive, member of the family. Its primary source is a **blog writeup** by Keller Jordan, *"Muon: An optimizer for hidden layers in neural networks"* (2024) [8]; the scaling analysis and weight-decay/update-scale fixes are in the arXiv **technical report** *"Muon is Scalable for LLM Training"* by Liu et al. (Moonshot AI, 2025) [9]. Muon (MomentUm Orthogonalized by Newton–Schulz) takes the momentum buffer of a 2-D weight matrix and replaces it with its nearest *semi-orthogonal* matrix — the polar factor, computed by a quintic Newton–Schulz iteration that runs stably in low precision. Geometrically, this flattens the update's singular-value spectrum: every singular direction of the update gets *equal* magnitude, so learning pressure is spread across all directions instead of amplifying the few dominant ones that raw momentum (and diagonal Adam) would. The scalable variant [9] adds two fixes that terminal-JEPA reuses directly: keep weight decay on the matrix, and RMS-match the orthogonalized update's scale to AdamW's (the `0.2·√(max(n,m))` factor in the champion code) so that Muon and AdamW can share one peak LR and one schedule.

**Why orthogonalizing key matrices specifically.** The champion architecture is a delta-rule fast-weight programmer [12]: it writes each `(cmd, obs)` pair into an online associative memory `mem ← decay·mem + β·k(v − kᵀmem)ᵀ` with unit keys `k = unit(Wx)`, and reads predictions back as `k_qᵀmem`. In a linear associative memory, capacity and crosstalk are governed entirely by *key geometry*: retrieval interference between stored pairs scales with `|kᵢ·kⱼ|`, and the memory holds at most `key_d` well-separated directions. All keys live in the row space of the four addressing matrices `W ∈ ℝ^{key_d×d}`. If `W`'s spectrum collapses toward a few dominant directions — which elementwise AdamW does nothing to prevent on a small, over-epoched dataset — distinct commands/paths hash to overlapping addresses, past outcomes smear, and the very mechanism that carries the held-out margin degrades on unseen systems. Muon applied *only* to the addressing matrices is pattern separation implemented in the optimizer: it keeps the key map well-conditioned and full-rank, preserving `key_d` usable slots. **Orthogonality/spectral constraints** more broadly (weight normalization [16], which decouples a weight's direction from its length to improve conditioning, and Oja-style row-normalization) are the "impose structure on `W`" cousins of this idea; the failed homeostatic optimizer below tried exactly those.

### 3.5 Weight averaging: SWA and EMA

Averaging *parameters* along the training trajectory is orthogonal to the update rule and targets generalization directly. **Stochastic Weight Averaging (SWA)** [7] runs at a moderately high, non-decaying LR so successive iterates spread around a basin, then uniformly averages them; the average sits nearer the flat center of a wide basin than any single iterate, and flatter minima generalize better. **EMA** of weights (an exponentially-weighted running average, as in Polyak/Ruppert averaging and Mean Teacher setups) is the online cousin, weighting recent iterates more. Both are attractive on paper for an *unseen-system retrieval* metric that rewards flatness. Two evolved impls tried this — `r6_swa_tailavg_hold.py` (uniform tail-average over a constant-LR SWA window, written back into the live params before eval) and the tail-EMA "consolidation" inside `r8_fastweight_homeostatic_consolidation_codex.py`. Both are in-contract (averaging never touches the loss, so it cannot cause collapse or leakage). Neither won here (Section 4).

## 4. Domain-specific findings: what terminal-JEPA's search found

The `optim` axis produced one of the search's clearest lessons about *context-dependence* (epistasis) of optimizer design:

- **Best hand-designed schedule.** `warmup → hold → cosine-to-floor` AdamW (β₂ tuned, decoupled ndim-split weight decay) was the strongest hand-built optimizer and remains the schedule backbone every evolved impl inherits. The decoupled-by-ndim variant actually scored *lower* than plain AdamW in one recorded comparison (0.3575 vs 0.4014, per `r6_swa_tailavg_hold.py`'s notes), a reminder that the "obvious" regularization refinement is not free.

- **SWA / tail-averaging underperformed.** Despite the flat-minimum theory [7] fitting an unseen-system metric well, stochastic-weight-averaging (`r6_swa_tailavg_hold`) scored below the incumbent in every context tried. A plausible reading: at a 1000–4000-step budget the trajectory has not yet spread across a wide basin rim, so the average buys little; the regime is under-optimized (Section 1), which averaging does not fix.

- **The first evolved-optimizer win was Muon on the fast-weights keys — but only in the changed context.** `r8_muon_key_orthogonal_addressing` (Newton–Schulz-orthogonalized momentum, RMS-matched, `wd=0`, scoped by shape signature to exactly the four `(key_d, d)` addressing matrices, incumbent AdamW+schedule everywhere else) is in the current champion genome. Crucially, it is a *strict generalization* of the incumbent: on any architecture without paired thin key matrices, the Muon group is empty and `make()` returns the exact incumbent AdamW+LambdaLR, bit-identically. This is the epistasis story in one datapoint: **a differently-targeted optimizer had already failed.** An earlier round's hippocampus-targeted optimizer did not beat the incumbent, and the homeostatic-consolidation optimizer (`r8_fastweight_homeostatic_consolidation_codex`, which imposed synaptic-scaling row homeostasis + Oja orthogonalization + tail-EMA on the key matrices, motivated by [13][14][16]) was outscored by the cleaner Muon approach. The orthogonalizing update only paid off once (a) the architecture became a delta-rule associative memory whose keys are the capacity bottleneck [12], and (b) the update was scoped narrowly to those matrices and left the proven config untouched everywhere else. The optimizer win was unlocked by the *architecture* win, not independent of it.

- **Homeostasis/consolidation framings are verifiable but did not win here.** The biological motivations are real literature — synaptic consolidation and elastic weight consolidation [13], homeostatic plasticity / synaptic scaling [14], and the conditioning benefit of decoupling weight direction from magnitude [16]. As *engineering*, in this project they were subsumed by the simpler spectral-flattening effect of Muon. Recorded as a stat, not a verdict: the homeostatic optimizer's specific mechanism lost to Muon's; the underlying idea (keep the key basis high-capacity) is exactly what won, via a different implementation.

## 5. Design guidance and open questions for future `optim` impls

1. **Keep the schedule backbone; mutate the update rule.** `warmup → hold → cosine-to-floor` AdamW is a strong, well-justified default for this budget; the productive frontier is *which parameter groups get a non-AdamW update*, not re-litigating the schedule.
2. **Make new optimizers strict generalizations of the incumbent.** The Muon impl returns the exact incumbent when its target matrices are absent. This guarantees no regression on other architectures and makes the mutation cleanly composable in the evolutionary search — a pattern future `optim` impls should copy.
3. **Optimizer design is architecture-dependent (epistasis).** Muon helped *because* the champion is a fast-weight memory whose keys are the bottleneck. A future architecture change (e.g. the chunkwise-WY delta memories, or an attention trunk) may move the bottleneck and invalidate the current routing — re-derive the target group from the arch, don't assume it.
4. **Open questions.** Does spectral flattening [8][9] help the trunk projections or FFN, not just the key matrices? Would a lightweight Shampoo/K-FAC [10][11] preconditioner on the small key matrices beat Muon's orthogonalization, or is equal-singular-value the right prior for associative keys specifically? Does SWA/EMA [7] ever pay off if the budget or the LR-hold is extended so the trajectory genuinely spreads? And can the homeostasis idea [13][14] be re-expressed as a *constraint Muon already satisfies* rather than an added mechanism? Per the project's culture, these stay live and get retried recombined in changed contexts.
