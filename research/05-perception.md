# Choosing the Eyes of a Latent World Model: Theories of the Frozen Text-Embedding Front-End

*Research report — the `perception` chunk of terminal-JEPA*

## Abstract

The terminal-JEPA world model never sees raw shell text. A **frozen** text encoder turns each command string and each command's output into a fixed vector, and only a sequence model is trained to predict the next-observation vector from an exploration history. Because the model is evaluated by **nearest-neighbor retrieval** of the true next observation against hard same-verb foils, the geometry of that frozen embedding space is not a preprocessing detail — it *is* the space the world model must be accurate in. This report surveys the main theories for designing that front-end: how sentence/text embedding models are trained (Sentence-BERT, contrastively-trained encoders like E5/BGE/GTE/Contriever, instruction-tuned embedders, general encoders like BERT/ModernBERT, and code-aware encoders), why pooling choice interacts with the anisotropy of raw language-model features, and why a *stronger general* encoder can fail to help a *differential* retrieval margin. It grounds each family in the literature and connects it to what this project actually measured: retrieval-tuning was the single biggest data-side lever (+0.043 from e5-base-v2), yet a bigger e5-large *lost* margin because it lifted the baselines as much as the world model, and truncation of long `cat`/`ls` output is a live constraint.

## 1. The role of perception in this world model

**The contract.** A `perception` implementation exposes an encoder `MODEL`, a `render_obs`/`render_cmd` that serialize a step into a string, and a `pool` that collapses per-token hidden states into one 768-d vector (see `terminal-jepa/evolve/chunks/perception/baseline.py`). A data-side re-encode (`evolve/reencode.py`) runs these over the whole corpus to build a new dataset root; the sequence model is then trained and scored on that root, with the retrieval space *and* the honest baselines recomputed per root so the fitness stays comparable. The encoder is **frozen** — no gradients flow into it. This is deliberate and is the same design choice that carried over from the project's earlier synthetic phase: pretrained features are a fixed substrate, and only the *dynamics* over them are learned.

**Why freeze.** Freezing a pretrained perceptual encoder and learning only a predictor/policy on top is a standard and well-supported recipe in representation-learning-for-control. Parisi et al. (2022) show pre-trained vision features, used frozen, are competitive with or better than ground-truth state for training control policies [19]; R3M (Nair et al., 2022) is explicitly a "frozen perception module" for downstream policy learning [15]. In LeCun's JEPA program the encoder produces the abstract representation space in which prediction happens [17], and I-JEPA instantiates this for images by predicting in a learned *representation* space rather than pixel space [14]. terminal-JEPA takes the same stance for text: predict in a fixed embedding space, never reconstruct tokens.

**Retrieval geometry is the objective.** The R4 eval ranks the true next observation against 63 hard foils by latent distance (top-1, chance 1/64). So whatever structure the frozen encoder imposes — which observations sit near which — is the ceiling and the coordinate system for everything the sequence model learns. If two genuinely-different `ls` outputs collapse to nearby vectors, no predictor can separate them; if the space is smooth and discriminative for shell output, the predictor's job is well-posed.

**The differential-margin subtlety.** Fitness is not absolute retrievability but a **margin**: `content_top1(WM) − max(retrieve_by_cmd, wm_no_history, copy_prev)` on content verbs (`ls`+`cat`). Part of next-observation predictability rides on shared cross-distro filesystem structure that a *lexical* baseline (retrieve-by-command) already banks. An encoder that makes the whole space more retrievable lifts the world model **and** those baselines, discovering nothing the search rewards. This is the lens through which every perception result below must be read: the question is never "is this space more retrievable?" but "does it help the *history-conditioned predictor* more than it helps a no-model baseline?"

## 2. Theoretical frameworks for text-encoder and pooling design

### 2.1 Why a general LM encoder is a poor retrieval space by default

Raw language-model embeddings are **anisotropic**: they occupy a narrow cone rather than filling the space. Gao et al. (2019) named this the *representation degeneration problem*, tracing it to likelihood training with weight tying, which pushes embeddings into a narrow cone [16]. Ethayarajh (2019) measured the geometry directly — contextualized representations in ELMo, BERT, and GPT-2 are not isotropic in any layer, with high average cosine similarity between arbitrary tokens [18]. Li et al. (2020) showed this directly harms sentence similarity: mean-pooled BERT sentence embeddings have pairwise cosine near 0.99, and cosine loses discriminative power in such a space [9]. Two post-hoc remedies target the geometry itself: **BERT-flow** maps the anisotropic distribution to an isotropic Gaussian via normalizing flows [9], and **whitening** (Su et al., 2021) applies a linear transform that centers and decorrelates the dimensions to an identity covariance [10]. Both improve retrieval/similarity without new training — evidence that for a retrieval task, *the shape of the space is as important as the encoder's raw quality*. This is exactly why a general encoder used off-the-shelf (the project's ModernBERT baseline) is a reasonable but unspecialized starting point.

### 2.2 Sentence-BERT: producing a single comparable vector

BERT was not designed to emit a single sentence vector; scoring a pair requires feeding both sentences jointly, which is quadratic in a corpus. Sentence-BERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) fine-tunes BERT in a **siamese/triplet** setup so that a fixed-size embedding per sentence is directly comparable by cosine, cutting a 65-hour pairwise search to seconds while retaining accuracy [1]. Crucially, they found **mean-pooling** the token states to be the best default readout — a finding echoed throughout the contrastive-embedding literature and reflected in this project's baseline `pool` (mean over non-pad tokens).

### 2.3 Contrastive retrieval encoders: E5, Contriever, BGE, GTE

The dominant modern recipe trains the encoder with a **contrastive objective** so that matched (query, passage) pairs are pulled together and mismatched pairs pushed apart — directly shaping the space for nearest-neighbor retrieval. **Contriever** (Izacard et al., 2021) showed unsupervised contrastive pretraining alone yields a dense retriever that beats BM25 on most BEIR datasets [5]. **E5** (Wang et al., 2022) — the encoder this project adopted — scales this to *weakly-supervised* contrastive pretraining on a large curated corpus of naturally-occurring text pairs (CCPairs), and was the first model to beat BM25 zero-shot on BEIR and to top MTEB at its size [2]. **BGE/C-Pack** (Xiao et al., 2023) and **GTE** (Li et al., 2023) follow the same multi-stage contrastive template with curated data mixtures, both reaching state-of-the-art at their release [4][6]. The mechanism connecting all of these to *this* task is direct: the encoder's training objective (contrastive alignment of related text) *is* a retrieval objective, and our eval *is* retrieval. That structural match — not model size or general benchmark score — is the property expected to transfer, and it did.

The intellectual lineage runs through dense retrieval more broadly: Dense Passage Retrieval (Karpukhin et al., 2020) established the dual-encoder framework in which queries and passages are embedded separately and compared by inner product [12], and SimCSE (Gao et al., 2021) showed a minimal contrastive objective (dropout as augmentation) both improves similarity performance *and* measurably flattens the anisotropy of the space [13]. Contrastive training is thus simultaneously the retrieval-alignment mechanism and the anisotropy fix.

### 2.4 Instruction-tuned embeddings and asymmetric roles

E5 and its relatives are trained with **role prefixes** — `query:` for a question, `passage:` for a candidate — and dense retrieval is inherently **asymmetric** (a short query about a longer passage). Instruction-tuned embedders push this further: Instructor (Su et al., 2022) embeds each text together with a task instruction, letting one encoder specialize its geometry per task without retraining [7]. For terminal-JEPA this raises a concrete design axis: a shell *command* is naturally a "query" about the *observation* it will produce (a "passage"). The champion recipe (`enc_e5_base.py`) actually uses `passage:` on **both** sides, discarding the asymmetry; an explicit asymmetric variant (`r6_enc_e5_asym_query_passage.py`) renders the command as `query:` to place it in e5's query subspace, which is contrastively aligned to the passage subspace that is the retrieval target. Empirically the two were **≈ tied** — the query subspace is usable, but a bare shell command is not a natural-language question, so it under-specifies the query; a predictive-instruction elaboration of the query (`r7_enc_e5_predquery_instruct.py`) scored below as well.

### 2.5 Pooling: mean vs CLS vs last-token vs max

Given per-token states, the readout choice interacts with how the encoder was trained. **Mean-pooling** tends to win for contrastively-trained and Sentence-BERT-style encoders because their training signal is distributed across the sequence and mean-pooling is the aggregation those objectives implicitly optimized [1]. **CLS-pooling** is the native readout only when the encoder was trained to summarize into that token — BGE uses `[CLS]` (hence this project's `enc_bge_base.py` pools CLS), whereas a general BERT `[CLS]` without such training is a notoriously weak sentence vector [9]. **Last-token** pooling is standard for decoder-style/causal embedders but is less natural for a bidirectional encoder; **max-pooling** discards magnitude information across tokens. This project carries all four as ModernBERT variants (`cls.py`, `last_token.py`, `max_pool.py` vs the mean baseline). The general lesson: pooling is not a free hyperparameter — it should match the encoder's training-time readout, and for contrastive/retrieval encoders that is mean (E5, Contriever, GTE) or the trained CLS (BGE).

### 2.6 General and code-aware encoders

ModernBERT (Warner et al., 2024) is a modernized bidirectional encoder — RoPE, GeGLU, alternating local/global attention, 8192-token context, trained on 2T tokens including code — and a Pareto improvement over BERT for classification and retrieval [8]. It is the project's baseline "eyes." **Code-aware** encoders like CodeBERT (Feng et al., 2020), pretrained bimodally on natural language and programming languages [11], are an intuitive fit for shell output and config files, which are code-ish. But CodeBERT is trained for code *understanding* (replaced-token detection, NL-PL alignment), **not** for retrieval geometry — and that distinction turned out to be the decisive one here.

## 3. Evaluation and the "bigger isn't better" phenomenon

MTEB (Muennighoff et al., 2022) benchmarks embedding models across dozens of tasks and found **no single method dominates** — the field has not converged on a universal embedding [3]. A high MTEB average is a weak predictor of performance on any specific downstream geometry, and a *differential* metric like ours is even less forgiving. Two mechanisms explain why a stronger general encoder can fail to help:

1. **Objective mismatch dominates size.** A larger encoder trained for the wrong property (understanding, not retrieval) can underperform a smaller retrieval-tuned one. E5 at MTEB matched models 40× its size [2]; GTE-base beat 10× larger models [6] — capability-per-parameter on *retrieval* comes from the training objective, not scale.
2. **Differential help, not absolute retrievability.** When an encoder improves the *whole* space, it lifts the no-model baselines (retrieve-by-command, copy-prev) as much as the world model, and the margin — which subtracts them — does not move. This is a measurement property specific to world-model evaluation via retrieval: only *history-conditioned* predictability that the lexical baselines cannot bank shows up as margin.

Both mechanisms are visible in this project's data (Section 4).

## 4. Domain-specific findings — this project's perception results

Recorded in the project's neutral ledger and README (content-verb margin; proxy = 1000 steps/seed 0, full = 4000/3 seeds; e5-base-v2 root vs ModernBERT baseline):

- **Retrieval-tuning is the helpful property (the biggest single data-side lever).** Swapping ModernBERT → **e5-base-v2** gave **+0.043 full-final margin** — the first genome above 0.50 — and drove world-model content top-1 from ~0.72 to ~0.80. This is the direct confirmation of Section 2.3: an encoder whose contrastive objective matches the retrieval eval helps.
- **BGE < e5; CodeBERT < ModernBERT.** BGE (also retrieval-tuned, CLS-pooled) helped but less than e5. **CodeBERT — code-aware but *not* retrieval-tuned — scored *below* the general ModernBERT baseline.** Together these isolate the causal property: it is *retrieval*-tuning, not code-awareness and not "a better/more-specialized encoder," that transfers.
- **e5-large *lost* margin — the differential-margin failure mode.** Scaling to e5-large-v2 (1024-d, projected to 768 by a fixed orthonormal map, `enc_e5_large_proj768.py`) gave comparable world-model top-1 but **lost margin**, because it lifted the objective-independent baselines as much as the world model. This is precisely mechanism (2) of Section 3: a stronger encoder that improves absolute retrievability everywhere earns nothing on a differential metric. It is the cleanest in-project instance of "bigger isn't better."
- **Asymmetric query/passage prefixes ≈ tied; predictive-instruction query below.** Consistent with Section 2.4 — e5's query subspace is real, but a bare shell command under-specifies a "query," so placing it there neither helps nor hurts on net.
- **Truncation of long output is a live constraint.** The renders cap output (`OBS_CAP = 1600` chars); e5's tokenizer head-truncates at 256 tokens, cutting ~29% of `cat` and ~15% of `ls` observations. A KMV-sketch `ls` render (`r8_kmv_sketch_render.py`) built specifically to pack more of the truncated listing into the head **hurt** (−0.0073) — and, unlike the e5-large case, here the *world model itself* dropped rather than the baselines rising. Long-output aggregation remains an open perception problem: the information is being lost at tokenization, but naive re-packing degrades the geometry.
- **Multi-vector renders.** Splitting an observation into structured segments (`e5_multivec.py`, K=4) is a stream-axis question; single-vector mean-pooling remained the competitive default, matching the Section 2.5 expectation for a mean-trained encoder.

## 5. Design guidance and open questions

**Guidance for future `perception` impls.**
1. **Prefer retrieval-tuned encoders (E5/BGE/GTE/Contriever family) over general or code-specialized ones.** The transferable property is objective-match to the retrieval eval, confirmed here and predicted by the contrastive-retrieval literature [2][4][5][6].
2. **Match pooling to the encoder's training readout** — mean for E5/GTE/Contriever/Sentence-BERT, trained-CLS for BGE — not an arbitrary choice [1][9].
3. **Judge candidates on differential margin, never absolute retrievability.** A gain that also lifts the lexical baselines is not a gain; this killed e5-large. Scale is worth spending only if it disproportionately helps history-conditioned prediction.
4. **Treat truncation of long `cat`/`ls` output as a first-class problem**, but validate any re-render on the world model's own retrieval, since packing more raw text can degrade the space (the KMV result).

**Open questions.**
- *Whitening/isotropization of the frozen space.* BERT-flow and whitening [9][10] improve retrieval geometry cheaply and post-hoc; whether an isotropizing transform on the e5 space helps the *differential* margin (or merely lifts baselines too) is untested here and is the natural next probe.
- *Fine-tuning a slice of the encoder.* The whole program freezes perception by design, but a light contrastive fine-tune on shell text — aligning commands to their observations in-domain — is the highest-leverage untested lever, at the cost of the clean "learn only dynamics" story.
- *Instruction-conditioned observation renders.* Instructor-style task instructions [7] were tried only on the command side; conditioning the *observation* render on verb/system is unexplored.
- *Long-context encoders.* ModernBERT's 8192-token context [8] versus e5's 512/256 limit is a real axis for long listings — trading e5's retrieval-tuning against ModernBERT's context is a concrete recombination the search has not run.
