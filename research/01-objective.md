# The Training Objective in a JEPA-Style Latent-Prediction World Model: Theory, Collapse, and Retrieval-Aligned Design

## Abstract

This report surveys the theory of how to design the training loss for a JEPA-style predictor: a network that maps an exploration history to a predicted next-observation *embedding*, trained to place that prediction near a target embedding, and evaluated by nearest-neighbor retrieval against hard same-verb foils. The central failure mode is **representation collapse** — the trivial solution of predicting a constant. We map the major theoretical families that a loss can draw on: (1) the contrastive / InfoNCE / mutual-information view, where in-batch negatives and a temperature supply the *relative-ranking* pressure a pointwise regression loss lacks; (2) non-contrastive redundancy-reduction and variance-covariance methods (BYOL, SimSiam, Barlow Twins, VICReg, W-MSE), which block collapse *without* negatives via architectural asymmetry or explicit variance/decorrelation terms; (3) deep metric learning and learning-to-rank losses (triplet, N-pairs, NCA, focal), which are the natural language for a retrieval metric; and (4) two cross-domain framings that this project's champion objective actually instantiates — **free-energy / predictive-coding precision-weighting** and **hubness / CSLS local-scaling** in high-dimensional retrieval. We then connect each family to this project's empirical record: the single biggest early lever was replacing plain MSE with an L2-geometry InfoNCE and then a precision-weighted contrastive loss, while an NCA-hinge variant and a Sinkhorn optimal-assignment loss underperformed.

## 1. The role of the objective in this world model

The world model is a causal transformer over an interleaved `cmd, obs, cmd, obs, …` stream of *frozen* encoder embeddings; only the transformer learns. At each command position the hidden state emits a prediction `pred ∈ R^D`, and the loss compares it to the true next-observation embedding `tgt ∈ R^D` (standardized). The `objective` chunk's entire contract is a single scalar function `loss(pred, tgt) -> scalar` over the flattened command-position tensors of a batch (`chunks/objective/mse.py`). Because the whole batch of targets is available inside `loss`, batch-level objectives — contrastive, variance-based, assignment-based — can be formed directly.

What "good" means here is specific and constrains the theory. Fitness is not the loss value but a **held-out retrieval margin**: on content verbs (`ls`/`cat`) the model ranks the true next observation against 63 hard **same-verb** foils by *per-dimension mean squared L2 distance*, strict top-1, and fitness is `content_top1(WM) − max(content_top1 of retrieve-by-command, history-free MLP, copy-prev)`. Two properties of this metric drive objective design:

- **It is a ranking metric in a fixed geometry.** The eval scores by squared Euclidean distance, so an objective that shapes *relative* distances in exactly that geometry is a differentiable surrogate for the thing being measured — a much tighter alignment than a pointwise regression that only pulls each prediction toward its own target in isolation.
- **It is a *margin* over baselines, not absolute retrievability.** A large share of the signal rides on shared cross-distro filesystem structure that the lexical baseline already banks; a change that lifts the world model *and* the baselines equally has discovered nothing (empirically, a larger e5-large encoder *lost* margin by doing exactly this). So the objective must produce *differential* help on the predictable content.

Collapse is the governing constraint. A constant prediction is useless but is a global minimizer of many naive losses; every objective here must be **anti-collapse-safe** (a constant `pred` must not minimize it), enforced both by the loss's own structure and by a hard calibration filter that maps predict-mean-≈-chance genomes to `−inf`.

## 2. Contrastive / InfoNCE / mutual-information view

The contrastive family is the historical anchor for latent prediction. **Contrastive Predictive Coding** [1] introduced the InfoNCE loss precisely for "predict the future in latent space": an autoregressive context predicts a latent, and the loss is a categorical cross-entropy that must pick the true future sample out of a set of noise samples. Minimizing InfoNCE maximizes a lower bound on the mutual information between context and target, which is the formal justification for why contrastive prediction learns *useful* latents rather than collapsing. This is the direct intellectual ancestor of a JEPA predictor.

**SimCLR** [2] made the mechanics canonical: no explicit negative mining — every other example in the minibatch is an in-batch negative — with an NT-Xent loss and a **temperature** τ that controls how sharply the softmax concentrates on hard negatives. **MoCo** [3] decoupled the negative pool from the batch via a momentum-encoded queue, showing that a large, consistent dictionary of negatives helps. Wang & Isola [4] gave the cleanest mechanistic account: contrastive loss asymptotically optimizes two properties — **alignment** (positives close) and **uniformity** (features spread on the hypersphere). Uniformity is the anti-collapse force: negatives push everything apart, so the constant solution is strongly penalized. Temperature trades these off; too large and hard negatives are ignored, too small and the softmax hard-maxes onto a single nearest negative and its gradient saturates.

This is exactly the lever that moved this project first. Plain MSE (the R4 baseline) never tells the predictor to make the true target *closer than the other targets*; it optimizes absolute placement only. Replacing it with an InfoNCE whose **logits are negative squared-L2 distances to all in-batch targets** — the same geometry the retrieval metric scores by — plus a small MSE anchor for absolute placement, raised the held-out margin 0.306 → 0.459 (`g1_l2infonce_claude.py`), the largest single objective jump in the program. Anti-collapse is automatic: a constant prediction makes every row's logit vector identical across columns, so the softmax is uniform and InfoNCE is pinned at `log n`. The temperature story from Wang & Isola then reappeared as an internal bug: the first InfoNCE used τ = √D ≈ 27.7, which for standardized 768-d targets makes logits differ by thousands and the softmax a hardmax with zero entropy — an accidental single-nearest-negative loss. Later champions use τ = 0.25 on the per-dim-mean distance, keeping the softmax in an informative regime.

## 3. Non-contrastive: redundancy-reduction and variance-covariance

Negatives are expensive and, more importantly, the *right* negatives are hard to define. A parallel family prevents collapse with **no negatives at all**, which is directly relevant because our target space is frozen and the true anti-collapse question is "does the predictor's output space stay non-degenerate."

- **BYOL** [5] predicts a slow-moving (EMA) target network's representation from an online view with only a cosine-similarity loss, no negatives — collapse is averted by the predictor + stop-gradient + EMA asymmetry rather than by repulsion. Notably, I-JEPA [11] and LeCun's JEPA blueprint [10] use exactly this EMA-target + stop-gradient recipe.
- **SimSiam** [6] stripped BYOL to its core and showed empirically that a **stop-gradient** on the target branch is the single ingredient that prevents collapse; remove it and training diverges to the constant solution. This is a warning for JEPA predictors: whether the target is stop-gradiented (as here — the target embeddings are frozen) fundamentally changes the collapse landscape.
- **Barlow Twins** [7] drives the cross-correlation matrix between two embedding sets toward the identity: diagonal → 1 (invariance) and off-diagonal → 0 (**redundancy reduction**). Collapse is blocked because a constant output cannot make the diagonal 1.
- **VICReg** [8] makes the anti-collapse terms fully explicit and decoupled: a **variance** hinge that forces each dimension's std above a threshold (the direct collapse block), a **covariance** term that decorrelates dimensions, and an invariance (MSE) term. Because the collapse guard is an explicit per-dimension variance floor, VICReg needs neither negatives nor asymmetry.
- **W-MSE / whitening** [9] achieves the same end by whitening the batch (decorrelate + unit variance) before an MSE, so representations are scattered by construction.

The lesson these carry into `objective` design is that **anti-collapse can be a separate, explicit term** rather than an emergent property of repulsion. In this project the anti-collapse guarantee is supplied cheaply by the always-present small MSE anchor (positive whenever a constant prediction meets varying targets) plus the contrastive term's structural `log n` floor, so a full VICReg-style variance regularizer has not been needed — but variance/covariance terms remain an unexplored axis for the frozen target geometry.

## 4. Deep metric learning and learning-to-rank

Because fitness *is* a retrieval metric, the most native theory is deep metric learning — losses designed so that nearest-neighbor retrieval in the learned space is correct.

**LMNN** [12] is the template: learn a metric so an example's k nearest neighbors share its label while impostors are pushed out by a **large margin**, via a hinge — the same margin idea as SVMs. **FaceNet's triplet loss** [14] instantiates this for embeddings: anchor–positive closer than anchor–negative by a margin, in a Euclidean space where distance *is* similarity. Triplet loss's weakness — each update sees only one negative and converges slowly — was fixed by **N-pair loss** [15], which contrasts a positive against N−1 negatives at once; N-pairs is essentially InfoNCE for metric learning, which is why the contrastive and metric-learning families converge here.

**Neighbourhood Components Analysis** [13] is the probabilistic, listwise version and the one most aligned with a strict top-1 metric: it maximizes the expected leave-one-out probability that a stochastic nearest-neighbor classifier retrieves the correct neighbor first — a smooth surrogate for top-1 retrieval accuracy. This is the theoretical home of the project's `l2_top1_focal_listwise` objective, which is a row-only NCA-style listwise loss on the exact squared-L2 metric geometry. Onto it is grafted the idea from **Focal Loss** [16]: down-weight already-solved examples so gradient concentrates on the hard cases — here, on the rows whose true target is *not yet* nearest, i.e. the ones at risk of a top-1 flip, via a detached `(1 − p_true)^γ` weight. This retrieval-metric-exact focal-listwise loss (margin ~0.461–0.463) was competitive with plain InfoNCE and became the scaffold for the champion.

Not every learning-to-rank idea helped. An **NCA + relative-margin hinge** (`g5_l2_nca_hinge_retrieval.py`) — self-calibrating the temperature and adding an explicit hinge slack past rank-1 — scored *negative* relative to the simpler listwise loss. A **Sinkhorn optimal-assignment** loss (`r6_sinkhorn_l2_assignment_codex.py`), which turns the batch distance matrix into a doubly-stochastic one-to-one matching (the SwAV [21] mechanism, whose Sinkhorn-Knopp assignment prevents cluster collapse) and maximizes the diagonal, underperformed badly (0.34 proxy). The plausible reason is a **metric mismatch**: the eval ranks each prediction against *many* true targets independently (a per-row retrieval), so the one-to-one balanced-assignment prior imposes a constraint the metric never rewards, diluting gradient away from the actual decision variable.

## 5. Regression vs. contrastive targets, and JEPA collapse specifically

Plain MSE/cosine regression to a latent target is the simplest JEPA loss and, when the target is a stop-gradiented EMA encoder, is what BYOL/I-JEPA use. Its risk is **dimensional / informational collapse**: the predictor and encoder can jointly shrink the informative subspace while still minimizing MSE, unless something (EMA asymmetry, stop-gradient, a variance term, or repulsive negatives) blocks it. LeCun's blueprint [10] frames JEPA as an energy-based model whose central design problem is *exactly* keeping the energy surface from collapsing, and lists the four anti-collapse regularizer families (contrastive, distillation/asymmetry, variance-covariance, clustering). In this project the target encoder is *frozen*, not EMA-trained, so encoder-side collapse is impossible — the only degree of freedom is the predictor's output distribution, which is why a modest MSE anchor plus a ranking term suffices and why the biggest gains came from *ranking* geometry rather than *collapse-blocking* machinery.

The decisive empirical finding is that **regression targets underperform contrastive ones here**: MSE (0.306) → L2-InfoNCE (0.459). Because the metric is relative-distance retrieval, a loss that shapes relative distances beats one that only fixes absolute location.

## 6. The champion: free-energy precision-weighting and hubness/CSLS

The current champion objective, `r6_free_energy_precision_l2_contrastive` (margin 0.5641, the best objective), is a cross-domain graft. It keeps the metric-exact row-only focal-listwise contrastive geometry of §4 and adds one principle from **predictive coding and the free-energy principle** [19, 20]: perception minimizes variational free energy, which for a Gaussian model is a sum of squared prediction errors each scaled by its **precision** `Π_d = 1/Var(error_d)` — the inverse error variance of that channel, which multiplicatively down-weights unreliable channels. Translated into the loss: estimate a per-dimension precision from the batch (detached), temper and band it to mean 1, and weight the contrastive squared-distance by it. The mechanism matches the task's structure: a **predictable** dimension has small error to truth and large error to foils, so it votes reliably in the ranking (signal); an **unpredictable** dimension where the model outputs ≈mean contributes zero-mean noise to the ranking decision. Precision-weighting concentrates the margin on the dimensions that actually separate same-verb observations — the JEPA "abstract away unpredictable detail" bet applied per-dimension. Crucially the weights are normalized to mean 1 so the overall scale still matches the *uniform* eval geometry, avoiding a metric-mismatch penalty. This precision tilt has since propagated into other objectives (`r8_voronoi_geometric_margin.py`) and, notably, made a learned *target-space* diagonal transform redundant (the objective already does the per-dim reweighting a diagonal target would add), which is why every pure target transform lost to identity.

The second cross-domain framing is **hubness** [17]. In high dimensions the distribution of "how often a point is someone's nearest neighbor" becomes strongly skewed: a few **hubs** are near-neighbors to many queries and repeatedly out-rank the true target — a documented retrieval pathology directly analogous to same-verb foils in 768-d e5 space stealing top-1. The cross-lingual retrieval fix is **CSLS** (Cross-domain Similarity Local Scaling) [18], which penalizes a candidate by its local neighborhood density so central hubs are down-weighted. The `r6_csls_hub_listwise.py` objective imports this: it estimates each candidate's local density (mean distance to its k nearest same-verb neighbors, a hubness proxy) and *raises the negative logit* of central hubs, making them harder negatives so the predictor must learn the within-verb *distinctive* directions rather than regress to the same-verb centroid. Empirically, hubness helped in some contexts but fusing it onto the free-energy objective (`fe-mutual-proximity`) was neutral-to-negative — an epistasis result: on the champion's memory arch the candidate-geometry reweighting is already partly supplied, so the added hubness term is redundant. This is a caution that even a well-motivated cross-domain principle only helps where the rest of the system does not already provide it.

## 7. Design guidance and open questions

- **Optimize the metric's geometry directly.** The consistent winners use negative *squared-L2* logits with per-dim-mean normalization matching the eval, not cosine/dot — the training surrogate should live in the exact geometry fitness is scored in.
- **Row-only, not symmetric.** The metric ranks each prediction against true targets, never targets against predictions; the symmetric InfoNCE column term is an off-metric prior that dilutes gradient. Prefer the metric's actual direction.
- **Get temperature right; make it self-calibrating if unsure.** τ that induces a near-uniform *or* a hardmax softmax both destroy the ranking gradient (Wang & Isola [4]); τ = 0.25 on per-dim-mean distance worked.
- **Reward differential help.** Because fitness is a margin, an objective that improves absolute retrievability of everything (WM and baselines alike) is worthless; focal/hard-negative reweighting that targets the decision-flipping foils is the right pressure.
- **Anti-collapse is cheap here** (frozen targets + MSE anchor + contrastive `log n` floor); the design budget is better spent on ranking geometry than on VICReg-style variance guards — though those remain untested.
- **Open questions.** (1) A learned, *jointly-trained* precision or metric (a full Mahalanobis à la LMNN [12]) rather than a batch-estimate detached precision. (2) Whether an explicit variance/covariance term [8] buys anything on the frozen geometry when combined with the free-energy tilt. (3) Whether a listwise NCA objective [13] with a larger, MoCo-style [3] cached negative pool (beyond in-batch same-verb foils) sharpens the hardest same-system foils the batcher already tries to densify. (4) Why exact optimal-assignment (Sinkhorn/SwAV [21]) mis-serves a per-row retrieval metric — and whether an *unbalanced* assignment recovers it. Epistasis is the recurring caveat: every objective's value is contingent on the arch, batcher, and target it is paired with.
